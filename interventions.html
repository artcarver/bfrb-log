Urge Surfing
ACT - 3-10 min

Not distraction. Deliberate, non-judgmental observation of the urge long enough to discover that it follows a curve - it peaks, then falls. Every successful surf teaches your nervous system what no description can: what happens after the peak.

1
Rate the urge right now
Mark where the urge is on the scale below before starting. You will check again when the timer ends.
Urge level - before surfing
2
Find where you feel it physically
Hands? Jaw? Chest? A pulling toward the behavior? Locate it. You are the observer - the urge is what you are observing.
3
Describe it without judgment
Sharp or dull? Constant or pulsing? "There is a tightening in my hands" - not "I need to pick." Observation, not identification.
4
Stay with it - start the timer
Don't act on it, don't suppress it, don't distract from it. Watch it. Most urges peak within 5 minutes.
5
Rate the urge again when the timer ends
The number being lower is the data your nervous system needs to learn the curve.

Grounding Reset
Distress tolerance - 1-2 min

For moments when the urge is primarily affect-driven - anxious, overwhelmed, dissociated. Use this to reduce arousal enough to then deploy competing response or urge surfing.

1
Feet flat, hands still
Feel the floor under your feet. Press them down deliberately. Hands flat on thighs or a surface. Establishing physical contact with something stable.
2
4-7-8 breath
Inhale 4 counts, hold 7, exhale slowly for 8. The extended exhale activates parasympathetic tone within 2-3 cycles. A direct vagal intervention.
3
Name 5 things you can see right now
Look around and identify five distinct objects. Say them quietly. This is an orienting response - it pulls cognitive resources into the present environment.
4
Name what you're feeling - one word
Anxious. Frustrated. Overwhelmed. Bored. Affect labeling measurably reduces amygdala activation. Not wallowing - a neurological intervention.
5
Decide your next action
Arousal is lower now. Deploy competing response, urge surf, leave the environment, or log it.

Stimulus Control Checklist
Environmental - Before episodes

Stimulus control reduces episode initiation by altering the environment rather than fighting the urge. These remove discriminative stimuli - cues that reliably set the occasion for the behavior.

Mirrors - apply frosted adhesive or remove from high-risk rooms. Reduces visual scanning phase.
Overhead lighting at desk - switch to side lamp or warmer bulb. Bright overhead makes skin texture more visible.
Bandage or finger covers on high-frequency sites. Breaks the tactile detection chain before scanning begins.
Something in both hands at desk - pen, textured object, stress ball. Hands occupied = detection phase interrupted.
Skincare routine at a fixed time only - reduces unstructured mirror use and turns face-touching into a discrete, bounded event.
Long sleeves or gloves during high-risk activities like studying or watching TV. Reduces surface access without confronting the urge directly.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation
Physical - 5-10 min

Most effective when the behavioral urge is driven by physical tension you haven't been able to locate or name. Works by systematically tensing and releasing muscle groups to produce a genuine physiological relaxation response. More useful as a preventive practice than an acute one, but can interrupt the arousal arc if caught early.

1
Hands and forearms
Make tight fists for 7 seconds, then release completely for 20 seconds. Notice the contrast between tension and release.
2
Upper arms
Curl arms up tightly (bicep curl position), hold 7 seconds, release. Arms fall heavy.
3
Shoulders and neck
Shrug shoulders up to ears, hold, release fully. Roll neck gently left and right.
4
Face
Scrunch the entire face - eyes shut, nose scrunched, jaw clenched. Hold 7 seconds, release. Jaw falls slightly open.
5
Chest and abdomen
Deep breath in, hold and tighten abdominal muscles, release breath and muscles together. Repeat once.
